There's a moment I remember clearly from early in my biology degree. Someone showed me a protein structure in a computer — one of those clunky old molecular visualization programs, everything rendered in bright colours against a black background — and you could rotate it, zoom into it, explore the thing in three dimensions.

I remember thinking: this is real. This exists inside every cell in my body right now, doing something specific, at a scale I cannot imagine, faster than I can think.

That was it for me. Proteins all the way.

## What a protein actually is

A protein is, at its most stripped-down, a chain of amino acids. There are twenty of them, and they're the alphabet. String them together in a specific sequence, and the chain folds — spontaneously, in milliseconds — into a precise three-dimensional shape. That shape is what does the work.

The part that still gets me, after years of working with them professionally, is how much is encoded in that sequence. The folding instructions are there. The function is there. The specificity — which molecule this protein will bind, which reaction it will catalyse, which signal it will transmit — all of it compressed into a string of letters that, in isolation, tells you almost nothing.

Reading a protein sequence is like reading a score without being able to hear music. The information is complete, but most of what matters only becomes apparent in another dimension.

## The diversity

Proteins are also, by any reasonable measure, absurd in their variety.

The same twenty amino acids, combined in different sequences and lengths, give you: enzymes that break down food, structural fibres that hold your tendons together, receptors that sense light in your eye, motors that physically walk along tracks inside cells carrying molecular cargo, antibodies that recognize specific shapes they've never encountered before. The keratin in your hair and the haemoglobin carrying oxygen in your blood are both proteins. So is the machinery that reads your DNA and makes copies of it.

This breadth is not an accident. Evolution has had a very long time and a very large combinatorial space to explore. What proteins have turned out to be capable of — the range of chemistry, the precision, the adaptability — is more than any single design philosophy could have produced. It accumulated.

## The folding problem

For decades, one of the central challenges in biology was predicting what shape a protein would fold into from its sequence alone. You could sequence a gene relatively easily. Understanding what the resulting protein looked like — and therefore what it did — required either crystallizing it (slow, expensive, often impossible) or, later, cryo-electron microscopy.

AlphaFold changed this. DeepMind's model, released in 2021, predicted protein structures with an accuracy that genuinely surprised the field, including the people working on the problem. The Protein Data Bank had taken decades to accumulate around 170,000 structures. AlphaFold released predictions for over 200 million proteins within a year.

I remember when the results came out. The reaction among structural biologists was somewhere between amazed and slightly unsettled — the way any field feels when a problem it had defined itself around gets solved faster than expected. The question shifted almost overnight from *what does this protein look like?* to *what does it do, and can we change it?*

## Why it stays interesting

I've spent most of my career on a narrow part of this — enzymes specifically, and specifically how to make them work reliably outside the cell. But the broader fascination hasn't faded, and I think I know why.

Proteins are a system where the gap between what we can describe and what we can predict is still enormous. We can name the amino acids in a sequence. We can, now, usually predict the shape. But the relationship between shape and function — why this enzyme is fast and that one is slow, why this binding pocket is promiscuous and that one is exquisitely selective, why one protein tolerates high temperatures and another denatures at the slightest provocation — remains mostly empirical. You test it, you observe, you build intuition over time.

There's something intellectually honest about a field where the experts are still genuinely surprised by the answers. It means the questions are real.

The fact that all of this is happening, continuously, inside every living thing, at room temperature, without anyone designing it — that's still the part I find hardest to entirely take in.
